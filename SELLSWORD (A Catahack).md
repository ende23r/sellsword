Medieval-fantasy ruleset for roleplaying wargames, set around ~~the not-quite-Black Sea c. 1300~~ not quite northern Italy c. 1400. For one referee and many players.

The map is composed of 6 mile hexes (3 miles on a side, about 23 square miles). Hexes have a terrain—such as flatland, hills, or forests—and a settlement score. A hex’s settlement equals its average population per square mile, typically given in multiples of 20\. Thinly-populated, near-empty hexes have ~~20~~ 30 settlement, densely-settled areas close to cities have ~~100~~ 300 settlement.

# RULES FOR WARFARE

The basic unit is the army. Armies are made up of infantry, noncombatants, cavalry, and wagons. Armies are composed of at minimum 100 soldiers, led by a commander—a player character.

By default, noncombatants add an additional 25% to the army’s total human count. An army of 1,000 soldiers travels with about 250 additional people: support staff, sutlers and their like, officers’ personal retinues, and other followers.

Armies may be split at a commander’s choosing, but bear in mind each new army requires a new, subordinate commander.

### SUPPLIES

Each infantry and noncombatant eats 1 supply per day. Each cavalry and wagon’s riders and animals eat 10 supplies total per day. By default, an army can carry 15 supplies per infantry and noncombatant, 75 supplies per cavalry, and 1,000 supplies per wagon. Armies in the same place may transfer supplies to and from one another, as commanders order.

An army can spend a day to forage its current hex and adjacent hexes (an approximately 10 mile radius). A hex produces foraged supplies equal to its settlement rating × 500\. After being foraged five times, a hex cannot be foraged again until next spring.

Foraging a second time within a year of the last carries a 2-in-6 chance of triggering a revolt; 3-in-6 if foraging in unfriendly territory. Revolts produce an army of infantry equal to the total settlement score of the affected region(s) multiplied by 3d6, led by a local commander. Revolting armies start with 9 morale but have a default resting morale of 6\.

An army can spend a day to torch its current hex and adjacent hexes, removing the possibility of foraging until next spring. 1-in-6 chance to trigger a revolt when torching, as above; double if torching hostile territory.

Armies with cavalry detachments may also forage (and torch) up to 2 hexes away (an approximately 15-mile radius).

Each day without supplies, an army reduces its morale by 1, then checks morale. After two weeks without food, an army dissolves completely.

### DETACHMENTS

Armies are made up of detachments, composed of soldiers of the same type (infantry or cavalry), often from the same region, plus their attached wagons and noncombatants. When dividing armies, detachments cannot be split. Thus, the 1st Aegyssus Chiliarchy (1,000 infantry) could be separated from the 2nd Aegyssus Chiliarchy (another 1,000 infantry), but each chiliarchy could not be divided further.

Commanders can transfer detachments between armies in the same place. When transferring detachments to an extant army with lower morale, check morale. On a failure, the detachments still transfer, but their former army loses 1 morale.

Detachment compositions vary by faction, but are usually 500–1,000 infantry or 200–500 cavalry. As detachments earn victories, commanders may bestow upon them names and honors.

### MARCHING

On roads, armies move 12 miles per day, marching up to 5 days per week, for a total of up to 60 miles per week. Offroad, reduce the speeds by half. Wagons cannot travel off-road.

A forced march increases this to 18 miles per day, marching up to 7 days per week, for a total of up to 120 miles per week. Armies of exclusively cavalry double their forced march pace. After a day of forced march, check morale: on a roll of doubles, lose 1 morale.

An army undergoing a night march travels 6 miles per night, or 12 miles at a forced march. At the end of the night, check morale: on a roll of doubles, lose 1 morale. If the road forks, 2-in-6 chance to take the incorrect path. Armies cannot night march off-road.

Marching armies stretch 1 mile of road per 5,000 infantry and noncombatants, 2,000 cavalry, or 50 wagons. Armies (or groups of armies marching in a single column) stretching longer than 6 miles travel only 6 miles per day, for a total of 30 miles per week, or 12 miles per day at a forced march (for a total of 72 miles).

When fording a river (rather than crossing a bridge), each mile of infantry in the column requires a half-day to cross. Cavalry ford at their regular speed. Wagons cannot ford rivers.

Armies march and camp in formations ordered by their commanders.

### SCOUTING

During the day, an army’s scouts see everything in its hex and adjacent hexes (an approximately 10-mile radius), including armies, strongholds, roads, rivers, terrain features, and other significant elements. An army with a cavalry detachment increases this to 2 adjacent hexes (an approximately 15-mile radius).

In bad weather, these distances are reduced by 1 hex, or 2 hexes in very bad weather.

Scouts can see other armies’ scouts, though these reveal only vague directions. A commander can choose to withhold their army’s scouts, or limit them to a certain radius. Without scouts, an army’s sightlines are limited to its hex only (an approximately 3-mile radius).

### MORALE

Morale is the will to fight and general spirit of the army. Morale moves up and down according to events: victory, pillage, and time off raise morale; defeat, bad conditions, and forced marches lower morale. By default, armies have a resting morale of 9 and a maximum morale of 12\.

Certain events call for a morale check: roll 2d6 equal to or under the army’s morale. On a success, the army holds; on a failure, consult the table below, using the morale roll as the result. In certain cases, morale checks carry additional specific consequences; the default results still apply.  
2\. Mutiny. 19-in-20 chance each detachment joins a new commander.  
3\. Mass desertion. Reduce the army's size and supplies by 30%.  
4\. 1d6 random detachments defect to another army.  
5\. Major desertion. Reduce the army’s size and supplies by 20%.  
6\. Army splits in half. 3-in-6 chance each detachment joins a new commander.  
7\. Random detachment defects to another army.  
8\. Desertion. Reduce the army’s size and supplies by 10%.  
9\. 1d6 random detachments depart from the main column for 2d6 days, then return.  
10\. Camp followers. Army picks up an extra 5% noncombatants.  
11\. Random detachment departs from the main column for 2d6 days, then returns.  
12\. No consequences.

When no other commander is nearby, detachments that desert form a new mercenary company. Appoint a commander for them.

### SPECIAL DETACHMENTS

Certain detachments have special rules. All detachments can benefit from a commander’s sound tactics.

Skirmishers are infantry, but act as cavalry for the purposes of scouting, foraging, and fording rivers. Armies of exclusively skirmishers ignore rough terrain, can travel off-road at full speed, and reduce their noncombatant count to 10%.

Heavy infantry count double for the purposes of determining numerical advantages in field battles. Heavy cavalry count quadruple.

Factions have their own special rules and detachments.

### BATTLES

When two or more hostile armies meet, the referee provides each commander with their current information and the lay of the land. Commanders draw up a brief plan of battle with their objectives, then each roll 2d6, adding the follower modifiers:

Positive:

- _Numerical advantage:_ ~~\+1 per 100% more total soldiers.~~ \+1 at 125%, \+2 at 150%, \+3 at 200%, then another \+1 per 100% for up to \+7 at 600%. Cavalry count double.
- _Morale advantage:_ \+1 per point of morale difference.
- _Chosen battlefield:_ \+1
- _Surprise_: \+1
- _Advantageous terrain:_ \+1
- _Tactics:_ \+0–3

Negative:

- _Rough terrain:_ \-1
- _Undersupplied:_ \-1
- _Sick or exhausted:_ \-1
- _Bad weather:_ \-1
- _Out of formation (foraging, resting, etc.):_ \-2
- _Tactics:_ \-0–3

The higher of the two rolls is the victor and achieves their objective; the loser does not. Then, compare rolls and apply the difference as result:  
0\. Defender, if there is one, holds the objective. Attacker, if there is one, loses 1 morale. Both sides suffer 5% casualties.  
1\. Both sides suffer 10% casualties. Loser loses 1 morale.  
2–3. Victor suffers 5% casualties, loser suffers 10% casualties. Loser loses 2 morale, victor gains 1\.  
4–5. Victor suffers 5% casualties; loser suffers 15%. Loser loses 2 morale, victor gains 2\. 1-in-6 chance loser commander captured.  
6+. Victor suffers 5% casualties; loser suffers 20%. Loser loses 2 morale, victor gains 2\. 2-in-6 chance loser commander captured.  
If a battle is impossible for one side to win (a \+11 net bonus or higher), the loser suffers an additional 10% casualties, but the victor gains no morale.

The losing army retreats 1 hex (or as appropriate), then checks morale. On a failure, it routs: it loses 1d6 × 10% supplies, retreats a further 1d6 hexes away (as much time as that takes) out of control, then regroups. If retreat is impossible, reduce the army’s size and supplies by half, spend 1d6 days out of control, and stay in place. Lost supplies may be acquired by victorious commanders.

When a losing army retreats, it loses the same percentage of wagons as casualties. Lost wagons and their contents may be acquired by victorious commanders.

When more than two armies fight in battle, allied armies must choose to fight together or separately. If separate, each makes their own battle roll opposed by the same enemy roll, adding their individual bonuses. Accordingly, one army fighting two enemy armies might prevail against the first but lose to the second, with each suffering their respective results.

If together, calculate each army’s bonuses other than those from numeric advantages, then average them together using the number of soldiers in each army to proportionally weight the average. For example, three armies fight together: one army with 3,000 soldiers and a \+2 bonus, one with 2,000 soldiers and a \+4 bonus, and one with 4,000 soldiers and a \+1 bonus. Fighting together, the entire massed army of 9,000 soldiers has a \+2 bonus, plus bonuses from numeric advantages, and makes a single battle roll.

### STRONGHOLDS

A stronghold is a fortress, city, or fortified town. A stronghold’s region refers to all hexes closer to that stronghold than another, with larger strongholds winning ties.

When under siege or assault, strongholds automatically raise small garrisons to defend themselves: by default, fortresses raise 100 infantry, fortified towns 500, and cities 1,000. In general, these garrisons do not leave their strongholds.

Commanders can garrison armies in a stronghold. They can also split detachments from their army to garrison the strongholds of hexes they travel through.

### SIEGES & ASSAULTS

When an army nears a stronghold, the referee provides commanders with their current information and the lay of the land. Commanders draw up a brief plan with their objectives, then the siege begins.

At any time, commanders can launch an assault against the stronghold. Assaults function as battles, but the attacker suffers \-1 to their roll, while defenders gain a bonus equal to their defenses. By default, towns add \+3, cities \+4, and fortresses \+5. If the stronghold’s gates are open, this bonus is reduced to 0\. In assaults, the losing side takes an additional 10% casualties. In assaults, cavalry do not count as double for the purposes of determining troop totals.

The garrison of a stronghold has a threshold. By default, towns have a threshold of 10, cities 15, and fortresses 20\. Each week under siege, modify the stronghold’s threshold, then roll 2d6: if the result is over the stronghold’s threshold, someone—a traitor or negotiator—opens the gates.

- _Default:_ \-1/week
- _Disease:_ \-1/week
- _Resupply:_ \+2/week
- _Siegers attacked:_ \+1/week

A detachment of 10 siege engines takes a month to construct and costs 1,000 ~~loot~~ coin. Once built, each detachment of 10 reduces the stronghold’s defensive bonus by 1 per week. After a siege, an army can spend a week to break down the engines and load them onto empty wagons. Deconstructed siege engines take a week to reconstruct.

After a stronghold falls, the victorious commander(s) must check morale or allow their army to pillage. When an army pillages, reduce the location’s held ~~loot~~ goods and supplies by half, the army gains 2 morale, and earns the ire of the locals. Either way, 3-in-6 chance commanders (and other important individuals) inside escape capture.

After it falls, capturing and controlling a stronghold takes time: fortresses require 1 day, towns 1d6 days, and cities 2d6 days. After the capture, the victors add to their noncombatant count: fortresses add 5%, towns 10%, and cities 15%.

Captured strongholds provide supplies, where X equals the number of weeks the stronghold underwent siege before capture. Towns provide 1d6-X × 10,000, fortresses provide 1d6-X × 1,000, and cities 1d6-X × 100,000.

### REST

A commander can allow an army to rest for a specified duration. After a week’s rest, armies reset their current morale towards their resting morale by 1\. At the end of a week spent resting in a town or city, commanders can pay 1 ~~loot~~ coin per soldier to raise their army’s morale by 1\.

In a town or city, each week of rest resets the army’s noncombatant count by 5% until it reaches 25%.

If a rest ends before its specified time—by will or necessity—the army loses 2 morale.

At the end of a period of rest, the army checks morale.

### ~~LOOT~~ GOODS & COIN

~~Loot is~~ Goods are carried like supplies. Goods come in multiple types, as determined by the referee.

By default, towns have 1d6 × 10,000 ~~loot~~ local goods and cities 1d6 × 100,000. Fortresses vary. Commanders in good standing can usually claim ~~loot~~ goods from their faction’s strongholds without issue; hostile strongholds must be captured first.

Coin can be carried, with 100 coins weighing the same as 1 supply. Goods can be sold for coin in land and sea ports; the selling price is higher the further they travel from their origin. 10 local goods can be sold for 1 coin.

### OPERATIONS

Operations are activities undertaken by individuals or small teams outside the purview of an army, including intelligence and espionage.

By default, an operation costs 100 ~~loot~~ coin. More difficult or complex operations may cost more.

The referee determines possible outcomes for the operation, then rolls 2d6. Safer operations, such as simple intelligence-gathering, have a high likelihood of success; more dangerous operations, such as assassination of a commander, have a low likelihood of success. Detailed plans and additional funds may change these odds.

At the referee’s option, they may call for a session of a preferred tabletop roleplaying game to adjudicate instead.

### SHIPS

Instead of armies, ships have fleets, with each ship functioning as its own single detachment. Some fleets have set player commanders, others are under the same command as an army.

Each ship eats 25 supplies per day. Fleets do not have noncombatants. On a failed morale check of 10, the fleet’s speed is halved for a day as sailors grumble.

A ship can carry 100 soldiers, 20 cavalry, 25,000 supplies, 10 wagons, or 10 siege engines. Warships can carry only half as much. Ships in a fleet share supplies.

Embarking or disembarking soldiers from a ship takes a day. A coastal hex allows up to 50 ships to embark or disembark; a river hex allows 25 per side. A port city allows up to 100 ships to embark or disembark, a port town 50, and a coastal fortress 10\.

Ships sail 120 miles per day on the sea, 120 miles downriver, or 60 miles upriver. Rowing adds an extra 60 miles per day, but functions as forced march. Sailing by night functions as a forced march, but allows up to a day’s worth of travel.

In naval battles, fleets of ships follow the same rules as armies of soldiers. Warships count as double for the purposes of determining numerical advantages. Warships with siege equipment count quadruple. Ship casualties include all passengers.

When a fleet transporting an army fails a morale check, check the army’s morale: if the army succeeds, the consequence does not occur, but the fleet loses 1 morale.

Fleets take 2d6 months to construct, and cost 5,000 ~~loot~~ coin per ship and 10,000 per warship. A port city can build a fleet of up to 50 total ships, a port town up to 25, and a coastal fortress up to 5\.

Weather can affect ships and sailing.

### SPECIAL ARMIES

Certain armies, like mercenaries, do not belong to a faction; others, like militant holy orders, are subordinates of one faction but belong to another or are separate; still others, like revolts, may be culturally part of one faction but politically opposed. These are special armies, which do not always follow normal rules.

Mercenaries are hired as an army on a contract of months or years, paid in ~~loot~~ coin. Mercenary companies consist of d4 \* 400 cavalry, plus 4 times their number in infantry. ~~They typically cost approximately~~ The mercenary commander must pay 1 ~~loot~~ coin per infantry per ~~day~~ month, ~~3~~ 10 ~~loot~~ coin per cavalry, and 100 ~~loot~~ coin per ship. If any mercenaries go unpaid, the whole army loses 3 morale and checks morale. Mercenary armies can be contacted in ports, as determined by the referee; after signing a contract, they require 1d6 weeks to arrive. Commanders of mercenary armies negotiate their own contracts with their employers.

Militant holy orders serve at the behest of religious leaders, but typically live in fortress-monasteries in the territory of other factions. They can be called to war by religious leaders as determined by the referee; in certain cases, they can be summoned by local political leaders as well. Commanders of militant holy orders negotiate their own terms and march from their own strongholds.

Other special armies may be present, depending on the campaign, faction, and circumstance.

# RULES FOR EVERYTHING ELSE

### TIME

Time is tracked one-to-one with real life.

As events occur throughout the week, the referee contacts commanders and offers them decisions: from there, commanders may issue new orders, as appropriate.

### WEATHER

Weather changes by the day. As the months change, so too does the weather. Weather can slow or prevent travel, alter rivers and roads, reduce scouting ranges, and otherwise affect logistics and tactics.

### CARAVANS

When the weather is good, caravans of merchants will arrive in major trading ports. They will offer shipments of foreign goods for purchase. Caravans consist of d3 \* 500 wagons, containing appropriate goods.

Buying all of a caravan’s stock will encourage more caravans to arrive.

If a port is blockaded or captured, merchants will avoid it until things calm down.

### MESSAGES

Through friendly and neutral territory, messengers on horseback travel 48 miles per day. 19-in-20 chance a dispatched messenger delivers their message safely.

In hostile territory, messengers on horseback travel 36 miles per day. 5-in-6 chance of safe delivery.

To send a message to another commander, send the referee a message, and the referee will convey it at the appropriate time.

Commanders in the same location may converse freely.

News otherwise travels roughly 12 miles per day overland. Along the coasts, it travels 60 miles per day.

### DIPLOMACY

Contracts and treaties are only as good as the word of their commanders. Assume commanders have sufficient translators.

Complex personal diplomacy may call for a roleplaying session with the referee.

Remember, you can always negotiate.

### SINGLE COMBAT

In single combat between commanders, each rolls 1d100 over their age: if both succeed or both fail, the more experienced fighter wins. Regardless, those who fail roll for death & dismemberment.

_v1.2, March 2026:_

- Removed most instances of “you” and related terms from the text (other than commander traits).
- Added parenthetical measurements in miles to most measurements formerly given in only hexes.
- Added regions back in as a rules term (lmao), referring to all hexes nearer one stronghold than another.
- Commanders raising armies must now explicitly stay within the region they’re raising in.
- Added a note explaining that heavy detachments are variable in numbers, and that future armies raised from the same turf are worse.
- Changed the math for revolts, now they equal 3d6% of the region’s population rather than a flat 1d20x500. Revolting armies now have starting morale 9 but resting morale 6\.
- Transferring detachments to another army with lower morale than yours now runs the risk of incurring morale loss.
- Renamed “Travel” to “Marching.”
- Forced marches now incur daily morale checks, and carry a daily 1-in-6 chance of lowering morale by 1 regardless of current morale. (No more 7-day quota tracking\!) Night marches work the same way.
- Scouts now explicitly see other armies’ scouts. Commanders can choose to withhold their scouts to prevent them being spotted, though this naturally reduces sightlines.
- Clarified language around morale checks and consequences.
- Specified that commanders include objectives in their battle plans.
- Impossible battles now result in extra casualties for the loser, but no morale gain for the victor, to make raiding more useful but more costly.
- Reworked multi-army battle rules: you can now either fight separately (maintaining individual bonuses but at smaller numbers) or together (getting bigger numbers but averaging together your bonuses). Thanks, DPR\!
- Clarified language around stronghold default garrisons. Reduced default garrison size for fortresses, increased default garrison size for towns and cities.
- Capturing and controlling a stronghold now takes time after the stronghold falls, with bigger strongholds needing more time to stabilize.
- Spending loot to raise morale now can only occur following a week of rest, and is limited to 1 per week.
- A detachment of ten siege engines now costs 1,000 loot to construct.
- Overhauled ship rules. Added fleets, warships, and construction times & costs. Overhauled the rules for morale, supplies, dis/embarking, and how ships fit into factions and armies. Increased transportable supply count.
- Added new section, “Special Armies,” for armies like mercenaries and holy orders, along with some default rules for those two.
- Removed harrying.
- Clarified language around when commanders earn new commander traits.
- Gave the \-1-in-6 chance to be captured from Guardian to Duelist.
- Ranger now means your scouts are less visible.
- Italicized a few random things that weren’t before, mostly in bulleted lists.
- Alas, the rules are now eleven pages long, rather than ten. I cry.

_v1.1, July 2025:_

- Removed regions as a rules term; armies now raise from all hexes nearest a stronghold.
- Updated wagons to carry 1,000 supplies to accurately reflect the spreadsheets.
- Removed “hire local guides” option in scouting; it’s been rolled into operations.
- Removed intrigue; it’s been rolled into operations.
- Added operations as a generic category of actions available to commanders at the cost of loot.
- Added harrying as a generic action, for armies wishing to engage without direct battles.
- In assaults, cavalry no longer count double for calculating troop totals.
- Adjusted the language around siege timers; they’re now separate from morale. Accordingly, adjusted the Brutal and Ironsides commander traits.

- Added rules for multi-army battles: each army rolls individually, the side with the highest single roll wins, but each army compares its results to the next-highest enemy roll (so a side could win a Pyrrhic victory, carrying the day at potentially huge losses).
